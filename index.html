Exploring and Knowing Exoplanets




Basic Facts:

(The basics about Exoplanets)


- Exoplanets are planets beyond our solar system. Most of them orbit other stars, while free-floating exoplanets(rogue planets) are untethered to any star. Some look like planets in our own solar system, while others are dramatically different.

- Exoplanets seem to follow the same general rules as the planets in our solar system: Small planets are rocky, big planets are gassy, and the ones in between may be watery.

- The word "exoplanet" comes from the term "extrasolar planet," which means that it orbits stars other than the sun, and thus exists outside of our solar system.




Types:

(How Exoplanets are classified)


- Rocky planets/Terrestrial planets; A rocky world outside our solar system.

- Ice giants: A giant planet composed mainly of elements such as oxygen, carbon, nitrogen, and sulfur.

- Gas giants: A giant planet composed mainly of gas.

- Carbon planets: Rocky planets in especially carbon-rich systems. They have rocks formed of compounds of silicon and carbon, different from our solar system's silicon and oxygen rocks.

- Hot Jupiters: Jupiter-like planets that are as close or closer to their sun than the orbit of Mercury.

- Hot Neptunes: Neptune-like planets that are as close or closer to their sun than the orbit of Mercury.

- Cold Jupiters: Orbit farther out in the planetary system.

- Cold Neptune: Orbit farther out in the planetary system.

- Lava planets: Superdense and are larger than Earth worlds. Have close and hot orbits.

- Super-Earths/Super-Terrestrials: A potentially/typically rocky world, larger than Earth.

- Mini-Neptunes: Gaseous worlds around the size of Neptune, or smaller than Neptune but larger than Earth.

- Ocean planets(in between Super-Earths and Mini-Neptunes): Have liquid-water oceans hundreds of kilometers deep.


Detecting Exoplanets:

(The five different ways Exoplanets are detected)

1. Radial Velocity
2. Transit
3. Direct Imaging
4. Gravitational Microlensing
5. Astrometry

1. Radial Velocity:

Orbiting planets cause stars to wobble in space, changing the color of the light astronomers observe. 1085 planets were discovered using this method.

2. Transit:

When a planet passes directly between its star and an observer, it dims the star's light by a measurable amount. 4156 planets were discovered this way.

3. Direct Imaging:

Astronomers can take pictures of exoplanets by removing the overwhelming glare of the stars they orbit. 68 planets were discovered using this method.

4. Gravitational Microlensing:

Light from a distant star is bent and focused by gravity as a planet passes between the star and Earth. 210 planets were discovered this way.

5. Astrometry:

The orbit of a planet can cause a star to wobble around in space in relation to nearby stars in the sky. 3 planets were discovered using this method.


Naming Exoplanets:

(How Exoplanets are named)


- The first part of an exoplanet name is usually the telescope or survey that discovered it.

- The number is the order in which the star was cataloged by position.

- The lowercase letter stands for the planet, in the order in which the planet was found(the first planet is always named “b”, with ensuing planets named “c”, “d”, etc).

- The star that the exoplanet orbits is usually the undeclared "A" of the system(useful if the system contains many stars, which themselves may be designated “B” or “C”).




Discoveries:

(Some Discovered Exoplanets)


TOI-715 b


It was first discovered in 2023. It is about 137 light-years away from our planet, and is a super-Earth that orbits a red dwarf sun. The exoplanet has a mass of 3.02 Earths and it is about one and a half times as wide as Earth. It orbits within the “conservative” habitable zone around its parent star, which is the distance from the star that could give the planet the right temperature for liquid water to form on its surface.



Kepler-10b


It measures 1.4 times the size of Earth, has a mass of 4.6 times that of Earth, and an average density of 8.8 grams per cubic centimeter (it is significantly denser than Earth's 5.5). At the time of its discovery in 2009, it was the smallest planet known outside our solar system. It is a rocky planet. It orbits its star incredibly closely, taking only 20 hours to circle it at an altitude only about 1.5 times the diameter of the star itself.



CoRoT-7b


It is 1.6 times bigger than Earth, with 5 times the mass. It was the first-discovered rocky planet, found by the French satellite CoRoT(Convection, Rotation and Planetary Transits) in 2009, using transit photometry. It has a global molten lava ocean surface. Scientists have even speculated that it is the leftover core of a gas giant.
